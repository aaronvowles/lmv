Why a Will and LPA matter

Benefits of a Will

  • Make sure your wishes are followed rather than the intestacy rules deciding for you.
  • Provide for your partner, children and loved ones; include gifts to friends or charities.
  • Choose executors you trust and set out funeral wishes.

Without a valid will in England and Wales, the law decides who inherits, which may not match your wishes—especially for cohabiting couples.

Benefits of an LPA

  • Appoint people you trust to make decisions if you lose capacity.
  • Avoid delays and extra costs of a court‑appointed deputy.
  • Give preferences and instructions to guide your attorneys.

LPAs must be made while you have capacity and are registered with the Office of the Public Guardian.

If you don’t have them

  • No Will: the intestacy rules decide who inherits; the closest relative applies to administer the estate.
  • No LPA: family may need to apply to the Court of Protection for a deputyship (application fee currently £421, plus possible hearing fee), which takes time and limits decisions until granted.

FAQs

Who inherits if there’s no will?

In England and Wales the intestacy rules decide who can inherit. Usually it’s a spouse/civil partner and then children, but cohabiting partners do not automatically inherit.

How long do LPA registrations take?

Registration with the Office of the Public Guardian typically takes weeks; start sooner rather than later.

What if there’s no LPA and someone loses capacity?

Family may need to apply to the court for a deputyship, which involves an application fee and can take time; an LPA put in place earlier avoids this delay.
